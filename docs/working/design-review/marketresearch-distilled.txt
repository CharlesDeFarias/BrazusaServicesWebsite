# Design Review Market Research Extract

Purpose:
This is a design-review input document, not a copy brief and not a redesign spec.
It extracts the parts of `docs/briefs/marketresearch.txt` that should shape how Claude or Codex evaluates the current live site.

Use this to review:
- whether the current design supports the intended positioning
- whether hierarchy and section rhythm build trust in the right order
- whether visuals are reinforcing or undermining operational credibility
- whether the page is helping the right buyer self-identify quickly

Do not use this as:
- a direct rewrite prompt
- a generic branding summary
- a source of unverified operational claims

---

## 1. Core Positioning Signals

- The site should feel like a locally owned, professionally run cleaning operations partner -- not a generic cleaner, not a luxury brand, and not a residential-first maid service.
- The core buyer psychology is reduced oversight, not cleanliness in the abstract. The design should reinforce: "I will not have to chase this vendor, re-explain standards, or wonder whether the work got done."
- Brazusa's value is the middle ground between cheap/informal cleaners and larger, rigid vendors. Visually, this should read as organized, mature, grounded, and responsive.
- The design should communicate order, process, calmness, and maturity. "This company is organized" is the target impression. "This company is trying hard to look impressive" is the wrong one.
- Premium perception should come from visible system quality: clear process, accountability, what is included, how work is checked, and what happens when something goes wrong.
- Identity signals like Brazilian-owned / immigrant-owned can support trust, but should not carry the page. Trust should be earned operationally before it is reinforced emotionally.
- The first one to two screenfuls need to establish category clarity, segment relevance, dependability, control, and proof. Anything delaying those signals weakens the page.

---

## 2. Trust And Credibility Requirements

- The strongest proof is operational proof: visible process, how service is checked, how issues are handled, and how communication works.
- Workforce credibility matters more than broad praise. If the page implies rigor, stability, or professionalism, the design should support that through structure and proof, not vague confidence.
- Accountability proof is critical: one point of contact, response expectations, and obvious ownership of the relationship are stronger trust builders than generic praise.
- Deliverable proof is high-value here: checklists, service examples, reports, readiness confirmation, and proof-of-completion cues can outperform most testimonial content if they are real.
- Testimonials are secondary proof, not primary proof. They should support operational trust, not substitute for it.
- Visual trust is strengthened by clean hierarchy, restrained motion, disciplined spacing, strong alignment, and scannable proof modules.
- Visual trust is weakened by clutter, excessive animation, decorative iconography, stock-smile imagery, and long sections that do not quickly advance the trust story.
- High-rigor claims should not be visually implied unless they are verified operationally. This includes inspections, verified completion, photo proof, response-time guarantees, backup coverage, clinic protocols, and turnover-specific guarantees.

---

## 3. Audience And Segment Cues

- STR needs timing control, guest-readiness, issue flagging, and low management overhead. Design should cue readiness, operational flow, and prevented problems -- not generic hospitality polish.
- Property managers / buildings need dependable support across units, schedules, and recurring vendor interaction. Design should cue coordination, repeatability, and reduced oversight -- without implying full property management.
- Offices need consistency, low disruption, and communication discipline. Design should feel routine-based, calm, and managed rather than urgent or deep-clean oriented.
- Clinics need a more disciplined and conservative signal than offices. The page should feel careful, restrained, and process-capable without over-claiming medical-grade sophistication.
- Homes / apartments can be present, but should not dominate the page's visual language. If home cues overpower the operator-facing cues, the whole page drifts toward residential maid-service signaling.
- Catch-all / other should feel intentionally handled, not like a leftover bucket. Flexibility is good; visual vagueness is not.

---

## 4. Design Implications

- The hero should feel stable, specific, and operator-facing -- not warm, generic, decorative, or lifestyle-driven.
- Early hierarchy should reinforce a control narrative: what Brazusa is, who it serves, why it is more dependable, how it works, what the client does not have to manage, then proof.
- The single-page structure should still behave like a routing page. Each section should help a serious buyer identify fit and move deeper into trust, rather than feeling like a long undifferentiated stack.
- The process section is not filler. "How it works" is one of the strongest trust-building mechanisms because it makes the control loop visible.
- Testimonials alone are not enough. The market trusts mechanism over adjectives, so proof should be layered across process, accountability, and deliverables.
- Section transitions should communicate increasing certainty: positioning -> segment fit -> process -> proof -> qualified next step.
- Visuals should avoid signaling domestic cleaning, luxury hospitality, or fake enterprise scale.
- If real imagery is weak or limited, the design should rely more on typography, structure, proof strips, trust cards, and process modules than on generic stock imagery.
- Motion should be restrained enough that it never competes with credibility.
- CTAs and forms should feel like a fit / scope / walkthrough conversation, not commodity quote-shopping.
- Mobile design should prioritize scan speed, hierarchy clarity, and reduced vertical bloat. If the page becomes too tall before it proves structure and trust, the design is underperforming.

---

## 5. Anti-Patterns To Avoid

- Generic local cleaning-template energy
- Residential maid-service cues dominating the page
- Performative luxury signaling
- Testimonial-heavy sections with weak operational substance
- Decorative icons, counters, or animations that do not build trust
- Large service dumps with weak segmentation
- Soft lifestyle tone or vague "partner" language unsupported by visible systems
- Any visual direction that makes the business feel informal, overdesigned, or unclear about who it is for

---

## 6. What The Reviewer Should Pay Attention To

- Does the first screen communicate "operations partner," not "generic cleaner"?
- Do the first one to two screenfuls establish who the site is for, why it is different, and why it is trustworthy?
- Does the visual tone feel organized, calm, and mature rather than domestic, flashy, luxury-coded, or templated?
- Is trust being built through process, accountability, and proof rather than mostly through adjectives or testimonials?
- Are STR, office/clinic, and property-manager cues visually clear enough to support fast self-selection?
- Do any visuals accidentally signal house-cleaning, luxury hospitality, or generic janitorial commodity?
- Does the page rhythm move toward qualified conversion rather than just stacking sections?
- Are any high-rigor signals being implied visually without verified operational backing?

---

## Reviewer Guidance

For Claude or Codex design-review use:
- Treat this file as a lens for evaluating the current live page, not as a request to invent a new style system.
- Separate true layout bugs from broader design weaknesses.
- Separate copy-density problems from visual hierarchy problems.
- If a criticism depends on an operational claim being true, flag that dependency explicitly rather than assuming it.
- Prioritize findings that affect trust, self-selection, scan speed, and perceived control.
